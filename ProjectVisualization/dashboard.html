Distribution of Numerical Data

×

Analytic Business Question

What are the fundamental statistical properties of our key business metrics like sales, profit, and stock turnover?

Analysis

  • Profit Margin: The histogram shows a strong left-skew. This means the vast majority of data points are concentrated at the high end of the profit margin scale.
  • Stock Turnover: This chart displays a pronounced right-skew, indicating that most products have a low turnover rate, with a long tail of products that turn over very slowly.
  • Unit Purchase Price: This metric follows a relatively normal distribution (a bell shape), suggesting prices are clustered around a central average with predictable variation.

Key Insight

  • The high profitability shown in the skew is positive, but it also means that any transaction with a low profit margin is a significant deviation from the norm and should be investigated.
  • The slow stock turnover for most items is a major concern. It suggests a risk of overstocking, carrying costs, and product obsolescence. The business should focus on strategies to boost sales for this "long tail."

Outlier Detection with Boxplots

×

Analytic Business Question

Are there any transactions or products performing unusually well or poorly that require special attention?

Analysis

  • The boxplots confirm the findings from the histograms. They provide a clear visual of the data's spread and central tendency.
  • Significant Outliers: The plots for 'StockTurnover' and 'SalesToPurchaseRatio' clearly display numerous data points far above the main cluster. These represent exceptionally high-performing products or transactions.
  • Data Range: Most metrics show a tight interquartile range (the "box"), confirming that the bulk of the data is consistent.

Key Insight

  • These outliers are not errors; they are your star performers. The business must treat them as case studies.
  • Actionable Intelligence: A deep-dive analysis should be conducted on these specific outliers. What product was it? Which vendor? What time of year? The answers could unlock a new, highly successful business strategy to apply to other products.

Correlation Heatmap

×

Analytic Business Question

How do different financial and operational metrics influence each other? For example, does increasing sales also increase freight cost?

Analysis

  • The heatmap provides a matrix of correlation coefficients, where 1.0 is a perfect positive correlation and -1.0 is a perfect negative one.
  • Strong Positive Correlation: A very strong link (0.96) exists between 'FreightCost' and 'TotalSalesRupees'. This is the most significant finding.
  • Expected Correlation: As anticipated, 'TotalSalesQuantity' and 'TotalSalesRupees' are also highly correlated (0.95).

Key Insight

  • The freight cost correlation is a critical strategic finding. It confirms that logistics are a variable cost, not a fixed one.
  • This means that for large sales or low-margin products, a failure to account for escalating freight costs could completely erase the profit on a deal. Pricing models and profitability calculations must include this scaling factor.

Vendor Contribution (Pareto Analysis)

×

Analytic Business Question

Are we overly dependent on a small number of vendors for our procurement (The 80/20 Rule)?

Analysis

  • This Pareto chart analyzes supplier concentration. The blue bars represent the purchase percentage of each individual vendor, sorted from largest to smallest.
  • The red line plots the cumulative percentage. The dashed grey line marks the 80% threshold.
  • The analysis clearly shows the cumulative line crosses the 80% mark with only the top three vendors. The top four account for over 90%.

Key Insight

  • The 80/20 rule is not just met; it's exceeded. This indicates a critical business risk due to high supplier concentration.
  • Strategic Vulnerability: Any disruption from one of the top 3-4 suppliers (e.g., a factory issue, a sharp price increase, or a competitor's exclusive deal) would severely cripple the company's ability to operate.
  • Recommendation: A strategic priority must be to diversify the supplier base to mitigate this risk, even if it means slightly less favorable terms in the short run.

Impact of Bulk Purchasing on Unit Price

×

Analytic Business Question

Does buying in larger quantities actually save us money on the unit price?

Analysis

  • This boxplot directly challenges the common business assumption that buying in bulk leads to volume discounts.
  • Counter-intuitive Finding: The data shows that 'Small' orders have the lowest median unit purchase price. 'Medium' orders, surprisingly, have the highest median price.

Key Insight

  • The data proves that the company's current procurement reality does not follow the 'bulk is cheaper' model.
  • Actionable Intelligence:
    • The procurement strategy must be re-evaluated. It may be more cost-effective to place smaller, more frequent orders.
    • This could have the added benefit of reducing inventory holding costs, storage needs, and the risk of product expiry.
    • An investigation is needed to understand why this is the case. Is it due to the product mix, shipping costs, or specific vendor agreements?

Profit Margin Statistical Comparison

×

Analytic Business Question

Is there a statistically significant difference in profitability between our best-performing vendors and our worst-performing ones?

Analysis

  • This chart plots the 95% confidence intervals for the mean profit margin of two distinct groups: top-performing vendors and low-performing vendors.
  • A confidence interval is a range of values that we are 95% certain contains the true mean of the group.
  • Key Statistical Finding: The blue and red confidence intervals do not overlap. This is a clear visual indicator of a statistically significant difference between the two groups.

Key Insight

  • Top vendors are not just selling more; they are operating at a fundamentally more profitable level. The difference is not due to random chance.
  • Actionable Intelligence: The business should treat its top vendors as a 'gold standard' model.
    • Analyze the business practices, product mix, and contract terms of top vendors.
    • Use these findings to create a 'best practice' framework that can be used to negotiate with and improve the performance of lower-tier vendors.
